A funnel is a sequence of steps visitors move through before completing a goal. In website analytics, funnels help you understand how many visitors start a journey, how many complete each step, and where people drop off.

Funnels are useful because most conversions do not happen in one step. A visitor may land on a page, click a CTA, create an account, verify email, choose a plan, and start using the product. Each step can lose people.

## Funnel examples

Common funnel examples include:

| Funnel type        | Steps                                                                     |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| SaaS signup        | Landing page, signup page, email verified, project created, trial started |
| Ecommerce checkout | Product page, cart, shipping, payment, purchase completed                 |
| Lead generation    | Landing page, form started, form submitted, meeting booked                |
| Onboarding         | Account created, workspace configured, first event tracked, invite sent   |
| Content funnel     | Blog post, related guide, pricing page, signup                            |

The exact steps depend on your business model and user journey.

## What funnel analytics shows

Funnel analytics shows conversion rates between steps. For example, if 1,000 visitors view a signup page, 400 create an account, and 220 complete onboarding, you can see both the total conversion rate and the step where most people leave.

This is more actionable than a single conversion rate. If the biggest drop-off happens at payment, you investigate pricing, checkout, payment errors, or trust. If it happens at the first product step, you investigate onboarding clarity.

## How to build useful funnels

Choose steps that represent meaningful progress. Avoid adding every tiny click. Track events directly when possible, such as form submitted or checkout completed, rather than relying only on page views.
