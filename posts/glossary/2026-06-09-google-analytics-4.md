Google Analytics 4, often called GA4, is Google's analytics platform for websites and apps. It replaced Universal Analytics and uses an event-based data model, where page views, clicks, scrolls, purchases, and other interactions are treated as events.

GA4 is widely used because it is connected to Google's advertising and marketing ecosystem. It can track website traffic, app activity, events, conversions, audiences, and campaign performance.

## How GA4 works

GA4 collects events from websites and apps through Google tags, SDKs, or server-side implementations. It can then group events into reports for acquisition, engagement, monetization, retention, and advertising.

Its event model is flexible, but it can also be difficult for teams that mainly want simple website analytics. Many users find GA4 harder to read than older analytics tools because common reports require more configuration.

## GA4 strengths and trade-offs

GA4 can be useful for teams deeply invested in Google Ads, BigQuery, app analytics, and audience workflows. It also supports custom events, conversions, and cross-platform reporting.

The trade-offs include complexity, privacy concerns, consent requirements, sampling or thresholding in some contexts, and a heavier implementation than simple privacy-first analytics tools. Teams in privacy-sensitive industries often prefer analytics that avoid third-party advertising ecosystems.

## Why teams look for GA4 alternatives

Teams compare GA4 alternatives when they want simpler dashboards, faster setup, cookieless tracking, EU-friendly hosting, open-source software, lightweight scripts, or analytics that are not tied to ad-tech.
